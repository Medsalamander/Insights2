Introduction¶

Data Description¶

This data collects information from over 100k medical appointments in Brazil and is focused on the question of whether or not patients show up for their appointment. The following columns are shown on the dataset and they include 'PatientID' is a unique patient identifier 'AppointmentID' is a unique appointment identifier 'ScheduledDay' details what day the patient is set up to come in. 'Age' patients's age in years. 'Neighborhood' details the location of the hospital. 'Scholarship' deatails whether the patient is enrolled in a Brazilian government sponsored welfare program. 'Hypertension' details whether patient has hypertension or not. 'Diabetes' details whether patient has diabetes or not. 'Alcoholism' tells whether a patient suffers alcohol addiction. 'Handicap' gives information on whether a patient has physical disability or not. 'SMS_received' tells if a patient got an sms reminder or not 'show' tells whether the patient showed up for a scheduled appointment or not.

Questions for Analysis¶

-How does gender affect a patients likelihood of showing up for scheduled appointments.

-Are males more likely to show up than females.

-Evaluate relationship between age and attendance

-How does age and gender affect attendance

-Does scholarship(govt sponsored welfare) affect attendance

-Does sms reminder improve attendance.

Time lapse bewtween scheduledday and Appointmentday

Edit column labels to lower case.

Also creating a new column to show day of the week may be needed.

Age column contains one negative age and may need to be dropped.

Renaming no-show column to show. This was done to avoid confusion. Also converting the yes/no records to 0/1 int.

Conclusions¶

Gender appears to have no effect on positive and negative attendance. The distribution by gender for show/no-show appears to be similar. The correlation coefficient between gender and attendance appears to be statistically insignificant. However, more statistical tests may need to be carried out to authenticate this result.

The age distribution for both shows and no-show appears to be identical as both are skewed to the right. The mean age appears to be like median age allaying fears of possible outlier effect. The mean age of those who showed is not much different from the mean of no-shows. This potentially tells us that age may not possibly affect attendance. The correlation between age and attendance appears to be statistically insignificant and may require more test for authentication.

A pairing of gender and positive sms-received against show/no-show showed no difference between the two groups. Both groups showed similar show rate and possibly shows no relationship between these two independent variables and the dependent variable. The correlation coefficient also confirms this assumption and more statistical test will be required in order to authenticate this result.

A pairing of gender and scholarship against attendance showed similar show rate between patients who receives scholarship and showed up and those who receives scholarship but did not show up. Same, applied to no-scholarship/positive attendance and no-scholarship/negative attendance. Correlation coefficient also showed a statistically insignificant relationship.

It is also important to note that correlation or the lack of it does not equate to causation or non-causation.

One important limitation of this analysis was that the scope was limited to just measures of central tendencies. In other words, we could not use authenticating statistical test to confirm the result.

Another potential area of further research would be to look at the impact of wait days/day of the week and how they potentially affect a patient's likelihood of making scheduled appointments.
